Slide 03
The chapter's question

Winning an election through organizing is one achievement.
Governing through it is another.

Can the relational infrastructure that carried the campaign — volunteer networks, coalition partnerships, leadership structures — be repurposed to govern?

What is gained, and what is lost, in the translation?

Pothole politics names the test: does the model survive contact with the most ordinary, granular work of municipal government?

Slide 04
Argument, in advance

Where we'll land

The Mamdani case suggests co-governance is a prior-conditions problem — not a relationship problem.

Inside-outside structure can be built fast (the Hochul rupture shows this).
But it bears weight only where the outside party is internally constituted to act.

The lit review will name that prior condition by reading Ostrom alongside Tarlau and Engler.

Slide 05
Case in chapter · The Hochul endorsement

The rupture that built the structure

Patrick Hunter in conversation with Grace, NYC-DSA co-chair

In January 2026, Mayor Mamdani endorses Governor Hochul as part of a deal on childcare rollout. DSA finds out a day or two before. The base — and the people negotiating with the administration on its behalf — feel blindsided.

What follows is, on its face, a near-censure. What it actually produces is a permanent inside-outside meeting structure that did not exist before.

Slide 06
Hochul rupture · 1 / 5

How it landed

Patrick Can you think of a challenge you were facing within the organization, and a choice that you made to deal with that challenge?
Grace When Zohran endorsed Hochul, it was really annoying. They gave us a couple — maybe a day or two — a heads up, but not enough for us to change any strategy or shift a narrative.
Grace, continued We had been building this campaign to tax the rich. Initially it was tax the rich to fund childcare. But he reached a deal with Hochul that evidently included him endorsing her. The administration wanted to frame that as a victory. The childcare campaign did not feel like it was a big enough step to really call it a victory.
Slide 07
Hochul rupture · 2 / 5

The pivot

Grace We also didn't want to be campaigning against Hochul and then have the Zohran endorsement hit. So we kind of pivoted there. We ended up dropping the childcare frame — now it's just tax the rich.
Grace But the target of the campaign had always been Hochul, and then Zohran tells us he's endorsing Hochul.
Grace This was in the midst of the NYSNA strike, where she had been issuing executive orders enabling hospitals to hire scabs more easily.

The endorsement isn't a free-standing political choice. It collides with two live campaigns the base is already running. The cost to the inside-outside relationship isn't abstract.

Slide 08
Hochul rupture · 3 / 5

The statement

Grace Steering committee had an emergency meeting to discuss. We wrote a statement that was basically almost a censure of Zohran. I kind of think formal censuring is a little fuddy-duddy, but it was a statement that was basically: Zohran made a mistake.
Grace As a courtesy, we sent that to the administration before we put it out, and they called us and asked us to edit it a bit.
Grace It was a tense discussion, but we did end up changing the statement to be less critical of Zohran and to just say, DSA does not endorse Hochul, Hochul is bad in these ways, she needs to tax the rich.
Slide 09
Hochul rupture · 4 / 5

Trust held, but trust wasn't enough

Grace One thing that demonstrates Zohran and his core team's commitment to DSA is that they weren't just like, "Well, fuck you guys." That sucked — that was a moment — because we trust each other. They knew we're not going to send out statements or censure him or critique him willy-nilly; they took that very seriously.
Grace And that's when we started having regular meetings, not only with senior administrative officials, which we do every week now, but also with Zohran himself. We meet every other week.
Grace I sat down with Elle, and she was like, "Okay, something is not working. The kind of inside-outside communication is not effective. We need to fix this."
Slide 10
Hochul rupture · 5 / 5

What Grace told Elle

Grace Elle or Zohran or someone made a comment implying that there was no world in which we would have agreed that he should endorse Hochul. And I said,
"There may not have been, but you also didn't explain your thinking to us. That's part of what has to happen too. Maybe we end up disagreeing, but we never knew what the trade-offs or the strategy or thinking was until you just told us what the decision was." — Grace to Elle, after the Hochul endorsement

The deficit isn't trust. The deficit is structure for shared deliberation before a decision is final.

Elle · 3 / 3

Prefiguring, in office

On primary night, Mamdani said the campaign sought, every day, to "embody the values of a better world and make these values true by our actions." Asked what that looked like in practice, Elle returned to a single canvass story:

"One of my favorite stories was a field lead who knocked on someone's apartment door and began their script and at some point was like, 'wow, that dinner smells amazing.' And they got invited in, and they had dinner with this random family that they were canvassing." — Elle

The prefigurative claim becomes a governance question: can the office hold that texture, or does it dissolve in the work of running a city?

The chapter cannot treat the Mamdani case as the first time. It is the latest iteration of a cycle, and its operators know it.

Slide 17
The Obama ghost · the structural lesson

What Tascha took from 2008

"Two years after the Obama campaign, I went to Pennsylvania because someone I knew managed a Senate race in the same community. The candidate that all volunteers were supporting was not the same candidate Obama was supporting. Community did all the work to get Obama elected but had no control over the product of the work — data, contacts, volunteer lists. He took control. They started from scratch. That stuck with her." — Tascha Van Auken (interview notes, with Zainab)
"Learning from Bernie: you need an organization that lasts before and beyond. Organizations last; candidates cannot hold all of that. When someone is in office, you need outside organizing." — Tascha

The mobilization-to-governance translation, specified

"It's really about pivoting away from the guy and towards the collective project."

Slide 22
Tax the rich · the test, named

Why this campaign matters more than its dollar number

It is the first sustained co-gov campaign after Election Day:

  • Run by DSA, not by the administration.
  • Targeting Hochul (Albany), not the mayor.
  • Six-month window to the April budget cycle.
  • Originally yoked to childcare; pivoted after the Hochul endorsement.
  • Mamdani's attendance at the first rally is itself a co-governance signal.

The press hunger for an inside-outside split is the asymmetry the chapter has to name.

Slide 24
Tax the rich · Grace, on the press asymmetry

Why this is harder than it looks from outside

"The reason we have to be so careful is the media wants the split narrative. The New York Times — we pitched them. We knew Zohran committed to the first rally, but he asked for me to come. The article was a significant splash, but they're always looking for moments where DSA, or the broader left, and Zohran are out of step, because they want to weaken both him and us." — Grace

Co-governance is performed in public. The press incentive is to read every difference as a split. The inside-outside structure has to absorb that pressure without producing the split it predicts.

Slide 25
Comparative warning · Michael Lang, post-election DSA call

"Strong, but also fragile."

"David Dinkins famously had the Rainbow Coalition — very rooted in labor, in Black and Hispanic outer-borough neighborhoods, in working, middle, and upper-middle-class renters. What Zohran accomplished is the next modern-day iteration of that coalition."
"Dinkins won in 1989 under a relatively similar set of circumstances — a Democratic constituency facing a very reactionary and polarizing backlash. Dinkins four years later ran in a rematch with Giuliani with almost the same coalition and lost very narrowly. The forces that tried to stop Zohran are never going to stop organizing. This coalition, beautiful as it is, is strong but also fragile."

The implicit thesis: a winning coalition is not the same thing as a governing coalition. The conditions that produced one will not, on their own, sustain the other.

Slide 26
Lit review

Reading the case
against the literature.

Four lineages, one synthesis, one scope condition

Slide 27
Lit review · 1 / 3

Co-governance has four distinct lineages

1 · Indigenous–state co-governance

Aotearoa, Canada: formal Crown–Iwi authority over natural resources (Mutu; Three Waters).

2 · Collaborative governance

Ansell & Gash 2008; Emerson, Nabatchi & Balogh 2012. Public agencies + stakeholders, consensus-oriented, technocratic.

3 · Participatory democracy

Ostrom 1990; Porto Alegre PB (1989–). Institutionalized citizen authority over municipal decisions. Cautionary tale: PB suspended in Porto Alegre 2017 after PT lost locally.

4 · Movement–elected official partnership

Engler & Engler 2022; The Forge. Durable relationships between grassroots organizations and movement candidates after they win office.

U.S. organizers borrow vocabulary from all four. They mostly mean the fourth. The fourth is the most undertheorized — and the conditions it presumes are exactly the ones the others address more directly.

Slide 28
Lit review · 2 / 3 · Tarlau

Contentious co-governance: closest fit, and its scope condition

Tarlau (2019, 2021): movements are most effective when they combine disruption with institutional pressure — simultaneously, not sequentially. Co-governance is contentious by design: with the state, and with allies inside the institutions.

This frame fits the Mamdani–DSA case in its first 100 days: Hochul endorsement → near-censure → biweekly structural meetings.

But the frame has a scope condition Tarlau leaves implicit:
Contention only produces concessions where the base behind it is strong enough to make it costly to ignore.

She identifies how an organized base engages the state. She does not theorize the conditions under which a base is strong enough to engage at all.

Slide 29
Lit review · 2 / 3 continued · the MST stress test

The empirical case Tarlau builds from is sobering

The MST (Brazil) at ~40 years and ~1.5–2 million members remains organizationally robust but politically constrained.

Agrarian reform settlements have collapsed from ~45,000 families/year under Cardoso and Lula I to fewer than 4,000 actually settled under Lula III — despite an allied government.

In July 2025, the MST published an open letter to Brazilian society:
"Lula, where is the Agrarian Reform?"

Contentious co-governance with an ally, producing diminishing returns. The variable Tarlau leaves implicit is the one Ostrom actually theorized.

Slide 30
Lit review · 3 / 3 · Ostrom, read properly

The eight design principles describe the prior condition

The standard misreading invokes Ostrom as inspiration — "local communities can govern commons!" — and drops her. The actual contribution is conditions for sustained collective action.

  1. Clear boundaries
  2. Rules matched to local conditions
  3. Collective-choice arenas
  4. Monitoring
  5. Graduated sanctions
  6. Low-cost conflict resolution
  7. Recognized right to organize
  8. Nested enterprises

A note on the translation: Ostrom writes about common-pool resources — irrigation, fisheries, forests — not movements. The application is analogical. But an organized base is structurally a commons: trust, attention, and capacity that members can deplete or steward; vulnerable to free-riding; requiring governance rules to sustain.

Slide 31
Synthesis

What co-governance actually requires

Engler: that movements should co-govern.

Tarlau: how an organized base engages the state — dual-track contention.

Ostrom: what an organized base has to be for that engagement to bear weight.

Co-governance = Ostrom conditions (internal) + Tarlau strategy (external)

People-first politics travels not because of charisma or social media, but where Ostrom conditions are met in the meso-level base.

Where they aren't, you get the appearance of co-governance without the substance.

Slide 32
What the case is a case of

Four interlocking claims, restated through the lit

01

Mass participation rebuilt under conditions of institutional decline.

02

Relational politics at scale — organized by narrative, structure, and real relationships across a diverse metropolis rather than ideological alignment or identity.

03

Movement–electoral fusion: organizing, public narrative, and electoral strategy integrated into a single coherent structure.

04

Organizational recombination: organizers translating Occupy, BLM, labor, and electoral lessons into durable vehicles operating at citywide scale.

Each claim should be readable as a hypothesis about an Ostrom condition being met at the meso level — not as a feature of the candidate.

Slide 33
Closing

What the chapter cannot yet resolve

A serious account of what people-first politics makes possible must also be honest about the conditions under which democratic rebuilding from below can arrest authoritarian consolidation from above.

Three open questions for the draft:

  • Can the volunteer base sustain mobilization through the Tax-the-Rich budget cycle without being absorbed into Organize NYC?
  • Does the new inside-outside meeting structure survive its first substantive disagreement (not procedural, like Hochul, but on a budget priority)?
  • Are Cea's institutional handles — community boards, Rent Board, tenant associations — being populated, or are they being staffed?

The case is a test of whether Ostrom conditions can be built in, not just inherited.
